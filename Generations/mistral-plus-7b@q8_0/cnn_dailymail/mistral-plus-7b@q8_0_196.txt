The study was carried out by researchers at King’s College London and published in the journal Psychological Medicine, reports The Mirror.

A team looked into 29 cases of paranormal activity over a period of five years. They discovered that there were higher levels of chemicals such as chloroform, trichloroethylene and tetrachloroethene in these buildings. It is believed the gases have hallucinogenic properties similar to LSD.

Dr Luke Keenan from King’s College London’s Institute of Psychiatry said: “These chemicals are known to cause hallucinations, anxiety, depression, paranoia and psychosis in healthy individuals when they are exposed to them at high levels.”
