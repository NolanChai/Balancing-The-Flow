(CNN Student News) -- November 4, 2013
A new study published in the journal Nature suggests that humans may have evolved to love because it helped our ancestors survive and reproduce. According to the study, when people feel love for another person, their brains release a chemical called oxytocin, which can increase their chances of forming long-term pair bonds and having more offspring.
The study involved 168 adults who were given a nasal spray containing either oxytocin or a placebo. The researchers found that the participants who received the oxytocin had more positive feelings towards their romantic partners and were more likely to engage in behaviors that support long-term pair bonds, such as sharing resources and protecting each other.
While the study's findings are promising for those looking to improve their relationships, it's important to note that love is a complex emotion that can be influenced by many factors beyond just biology. Cultural norms, personal beliefs, and individual experiences can all play a role in shaping our romantic relationships, and it's unlikely that any one factor will explain the full range of human loving behavior.
Despite these limitations, the study provides valuable insights into the biological basis of love and suggests that oxytocin may play an important role in fostering long-term pair bonds. As our understanding of the biology of love continues to grow, we may be able to develop new strategies for building stronger, more resilient relationships.
