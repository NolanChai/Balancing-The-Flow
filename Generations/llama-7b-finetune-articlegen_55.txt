Her remains were first discovered in 2011, as the Beit Shearim National Park prepared to open a new visitor centre. It is thought that the young woman may have been buried at the site because of her Jewish faith. Beit Shearim is famous for its many tombs, which are all considered holy to Jews after it was first established as a burial ground more than 2,000 years ago. It's also believed to be where many Jews who committed suicide were buried during the second and third century A.D., in response to Roman rule. Beit Shearim is also a significant site for Christians because of its role in the story of Jesus, according to TAU archaeologists. Scroll down for video . 'Because of its location, it has always been important. The site is rich with historical meaning for three different religions,' said Dr Mor Agranat-Tamir, a lecturer at TAU's Department of Archaeology and Ancient Near Eastern Cultures. 'This young woman could have come from any one of these groups or even all of them. This is the face of someone who was part of this world.' Beit Shearim National Park has been used for burials since before the Roman Empire. However, it wasn't until a Christian leader named Nehemiah established his headquarters there in 200 A.D. that the site became popular among Jewish people too. 'He and other leaders came here because they believed it was holy ground,' Dr Agranat-Tamir said . The remains of Beit Shearim National Park's famous visitors were found at the site long ago, but many of the tombs themselves have since disappeared or been destroyed by erosion. However, their locations are marked with white crosses and it is believed they all contain significant artefacts - including personal possessions such as jewellery - which experts hope to find in future excavations.
